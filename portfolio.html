PPEngine: Refactoring of a peer-to-peer video streaming system

Jan 2010 - present, The George Washington University, Washington DC

Originated in my hands-on projects for my coursework at The George Washington University, I did refactoring of a peer-to-peer (P2P) video streaming system on code, design, and architecture level. Functions involving peer-to-peer communications between devices behind NAT (Network Address Translation) were moved into a separate software framework (PPIO). Video streaming and peer-to-peer overlay networking were also re-designed to realize a kind of independency.

Systems Architecture

PPIO is a software framework to ease the programming of network communications involving devices behind NAT. A service system (a single server or a distributed network) consists of devices in public domain (with public IP addresses) is used as a helper to make devices behind NAT reachable. The procedure to establish peer-to-peer connections is wrapped in PPIO.

PPStream is designed as a local helper service that downloads video contents with multiple ways to ensure playback quality. The video data (RTP packets) is stored in a buffer first and then fed to the player with RTSP protocol or alternatives. Different from traditional stream media system, in which all clients connect to the dedicated server and download media contents, PPStream works in a peer-to-peer fashion. At most cases, PPStream nodes connect to each other directly, exchange their buffering information, and propagate video contents as needed. Accessing server occurs only if the data is urgent as to a time window of playback. A PPStream node keeps track on a small sub-set of all nodes in the system, organizes them into three layers of Members, Neighbors, and Partners according to the possibility of sharing data with them. Buffering information of a node is packeted into a message and propagated among nodes with a Gossip-like protocol that is optimized for media streaming applications. Please find source code and documents at Github.

Compiler and debugger: Xcode4, Eclipse/CDT, Visual Studio 2008
Techniques: TCP/UDP, Socket, Thread Pool, OOD/OOP, XML, NAT traversal

FireWind: A peer-to-peer video streaming system

Jun 2006 - Apr 2009, QQ Technology Inc., Beijing China

QQ Technology is a system developer and services provider for carrier-grade broad-band assurance. It gained the investments from IDG Technology Venture Investment and Mitsui Ventures. I joined QQ Technology in June 2006 as a Project Director and Software Architect to lead developing a peer-to-peer (P2P) video streaming system and the operations support system (OSS).

About the system

FireWind P2P video streaming system is an integrable solution for video broadcast (TV) and video-on-demand (VOD) over Internet. Compressed video data is extracted from video files or real-time video streams by dedicated media servers, wrapped into slice packets, then fed into a peer-to-peer overlay network consists of users. The sliced video data are propagated among users on the need basis. The structure of the peer-to-peer overlay network and the strategy of sharing data are designed for the special needs of video streaming to ensure quick start and smooth playback. They are different from those in typical file sharing applications like Bittorrent.

FireWind IPTV system consists of P2P video streaming system and the operations support system (OSS). Its back end includes a contents publishing system and a video advertisements management system. Its front end includes a web portal (WebTV) and a client software running on HTPC (Home Theater PC).

GEV/DVx: A software framework for digital video applications

Jan 2005 - May 2006, Golden Vision Technology Co. Ltd., Beijing China

In early 2005, my employer Golden Eye Technology was reorganized as Golden Vision Technology. I was entitled as a Project Manager and Software Architect to lead developing a software framework for digital video applications. The design and implementation is based on reconstructing and refactoring of a existing digital video surveillance system (GoldenEye).

About the system

Safeguarding system was expected to be a part of enterprise information architecture. Current digital video surveillance systems were not easy to integrate with management information system (MIS). GEV/DVx tried to construct a new software framework for digital video applications, in which the devices, data, work flows, work log, even states are treated as resources under the information architecture. To be simple, GEV/DVx was designed to support the networking, intelligence, and informatization of safeguarding system based on digital video surveillance.

Functionally, GEV/DVx consists of two parts. Application management information system (MIS) and real-time digital video processing system. The management of hardware and software, users, data, log, and states are based on typical MIS architecture, which make it easy to integrate with other applications. Digital video processing system is a DirectShow-like framework, which concentrates on media-related work, including video capture and display, record and transfer, state control and actions, audio and video detection and analysis. Functions are wrapped into filters that can be configured to connect to each other to complete requirement functions.

GoldenEye: A digital video surveillance system

Nov 2002 - Dec 2004, Golden Eye Technology Co. Ltd., Beijing China

Golden Eye Technology developed the first PC-based DVR (Digital Video Recorder) in China in 1997 and successfully used in digital video surveillance system. I joined Golden Eye Technology in November 2002 as a Software Engineer (C++ Programmer) in a software development team. My task is software maintenance, as well as some improving development.

About the product

From the middle of 1990s, digital video surveillance system was used in safeguarding applications to upgrade previous CCTV (Closed-circuit Television) system. GoldenEye digital video surveillance system was one of the first products that dominated Chinese market. GoldenEye is a PC-based system that makes it very easy to evolve with the needs of market and the progress of technology.

Early versions of GoldenEye system worked in a local mode. The system core is DVR (Digital Video Recorder). Multiple videos are connected into local surveillance center via cables, captured and compressed with DVR, then recorded into disk files. Usually, the surveillance center equipped with a video monitors array (or display wall). The system provides interface to control the displaying and recording of the videos. In addition, the system may integrate functions like alarms I/O, video motion or audio silence detection, timing actions, or actions driven by events.

The broad-band networks were widely deployed from the last of 1990s, which together with the advancing of digital video compression techniques, made it possible to transmit multiple videos over networks. Functions like remote video surveillance were introduced into digital video surveillance system. The video compressed with MPEG-1 or H.261/263 standards can be transmitted from local surveillance center to remote users in the form of stream media. The users may watch one to four videos remotely over Intranet, even Internet.

The rapid growth of Internet band-width and high-efficient video compress standards like MPEG-4 and H.264 make it possible to transmit high-quality videos over Internet. To support larger scale video surveillance, the surveillance center of the new system is not set on local any more. Videos in a large geographical range are captured and compressed locally, then transmitted to the surveillance center over network (or Internet). The videos are recorded into disk files or decompressed and displayed on monitors. This kind of video surveillance system is named NVR (Network Video Recorder).
